I also got a weird feeling from Kingsolver's portrayal of Native American and Hispanic culture. With The Poisonwood Bible, you can almost tell that she was drawing on personal experience of living in the Republic of Congo. I don’t think its wrong for Kingsolver to use her platform to advocate for the types of issues she writes about in this book, but it just wasn’t as immersive as a story since it clearly lacked the details that come from lived experience. It felt contrived.

Lolita

Vladimir Nabokov

The prose is spectacular; a bitter, disgusting tragedy unreliably narrated by a charming perpetrator.

Unfortunately the film adaptations and publishing industry (misleading covers) failed in portraying the complexity of the novel, leading to horrific romanticization of abhorrent themes.

Personally I think it was Nabokov's goal to show how eloquent some monsters can be and how depraved we all are for falling for it, especially when you consider claims by some that Nabokov himself may have been sexually abused by his wealthy uncle.

SPOILER: It has always been my take that Humbert murdered Charlotte. Nabokov wouldn't be so sloppy with his plot to make a convenient death like that, and Humbert constantly fantasized about her death in very specific ways. The ambiguity is part of the appeal though.

I could write a lot in this review, but thinking about the book and society's reception of it for too long makes me want to throw up.

As I Lay Dying

William Faulkner

Absolutely amazing, genius execution of stream of consciousness and symbolism. Every single line has at least one but usually several words that individually strike you as masterfully chosen.

Faulkner has an almost inhuman sense of time and space; seriously consider this quote in the context of Einstein's spacetime continuum:

"It is as though the space between us were time: an irrevocable quality. It is as though time, no longer running straight before us in a diminishing line, now runs parallel between us like a looping string, the distance being the doubling accretion of the thread and not the interval between."

The James Franco film adaptation was great as well.

In my top 5 favorites (maybe, it's hard to pick). Somehow the Sound and the Fury is even better though.

"My mother is a fish."

The Sound and the Fury

William Faulkner

I say this a lot, but this one actually takes the cake as my favorite book. I can’t even articulate how incredible it is but I’ll try. The most impressive aspect is the truly innovative structure, the non-linear sense of time artfully mixed in with stream of consciousness is just breathtaking. Phenomenal writing style as described in the review of As I Lay Dying now even further improved by the addition of creative risks.

Hunger

Knut Hamsun

Skinny legend. Norway's favorite book according to an infographic I saw one time, which is a bit odd Hamsun was a horrible, horrible person.

I struggled to figure out how many stars to put for this book, because it is really well written and interesting. But art doesn’t exist in a vacuum, and to truly show how bad Hamsun was, here’s some context: He wrote a eulogy for Hitler in 1945, describing him as "a warrior for mankind and a prophet of the gospel of justice for all nations." Sometimes people say consider the time and separate the art from the artist, but in a case as dangerous and disgusting as this, I don’t think that’s appropriate. When we celebrate the work of someone with abhorrent views, we risk legitimizing their worldview or diminishing the seriousness of their actions.
